# Banking Exams — Interview Preparation

## Common Interview Questions for Bank PO

**Q: Why do you want to join banking sector?**
Best Answer: "Banking is the backbone of a nation's economy. I'm drawn to its dual role — serving individual customers while contributing to economic growth through credit deployment. The dynamic regulatory environment and the opportunity to work with diverse sectors of the economy make banking intellectually stimulating. I'm particularly interested in contributing to financial inclusion initiatives."

**Q: What is the current Repo Rate?**
Always know the latest RBI monetary policy rate. As of recent policy: Repo Rate, Reverse Repo Rate, CRR, SLR values. Explain implications: higher repo rate → banks pay more to borrow from RBI → home loan, car loan rates increase → reduce inflation.

**Q: What is NPA and how do banks deal with it?**
NPA = Non-Performing Asset. A loan becomes NPA when interest or principal is overdue for 90+ days. Classification: Sub-standard (< 12 months NPA), Doubtful (> 12 months), Loss (uncollectable). Resolution: SARFAESI Act allows banks to seize assets without court intervention. IBC (Insolvency & Bankruptcy Code) for large corporate NPAs. Banks maintain provisions against NPAs — reduces net profit.

**Q: Explain UPI and how it works.**
UPI = Unified Payments Interface, NPCI's real-time payment system. Works 24×7 using Virtual Payment Address (VPA). User links bank account to UPI app (GPay, PhonePe, BHIM). Transaction: sender → VPA → UPI switch → NPCI → bank → recipient. Supports P2P, P2M payments. Daily limit: ₹1 lakh per transaction. India now world's largest UPI market with 10B+ transactions/month.

**Q: What is CASA Ratio and its significance?**
CASA = Current Account + Savings Account deposits. Higher CASA → bank raises funds at lower cost (savings rates ~3-4%, current accounts often 0%). Lower cost of funds → better NIM (Net Interest Margin) → higher profitability. SBI/HDFC have CASA ~45-50%, making them cost-efficient. Banks aggressively market zero-balance accounts to boost CASA.

**Q: What is Monetary Policy and how does it work in India?**
Monetary Policy = RBI's tool to manage money supply and credit in the economy. Objective: price stability while supporting growth. Tools: Repo Rate (main signal), CRR (mandatory cash reserve), SLR (mandatory bond holding), Open Market Operations (buy/sell govt bonds), Market Stabilization Scheme. MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) — 6 members, meets every 2 months. Decision: majority vote; RBI Governor has casting vote.

**Q: Difference between NEFT, RTGS, IMPS.**
NEFT: batch processing every 30 min, 24×7, no minimum amount, for regular transfers.
RTGS: real-time, minimum ₹2 lakh, for large value transfers, 24×7.
IMPS: instant, 24×7×365, up to ₹5 lakh per transaction, mobile/internet.

**Q: What is the significance of CRR?**
CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio): banks must keep X% of their Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL) as cash with RBI. Purpose: ensure liquidity, RBI's tool to control money supply. If RBI increases CRR → less money with banks → less lending → reduced money supply (anti-inflationary). Banks earn no interest on CRR balances. Currently around 4.5% (check latest).

**Q: How would you handle an angry customer?**
LEAP framework: Listen actively without interrupting. Empathize ("I understand your frustration"). Ask clarifying questions to understand the issue. Problem-solve by offering solutions within your authority; escalate if needed. Never argue. Document the complaint. Follow up to ensure resolution. The customer's perception of the bank depends on your response.

**Q: What is a Non-Banking Financial Company (NBFC)?**
A: NBFCs are companies registered under the Companies Act that provide financial services similar to banks but cannot accept demand deposits or issue cheques. Types: NBFC-MFI (microfinance), NBFC-AFC (asset finance), Core Investment Companies, Housing Finance Companies. Regulated by RBI. Key difference from banks: no deposit insurance (DICGC), cannot create credit like banks, higher interest rates typically. Examples: Bajaj Finance, LIC Housing Finance, Muthoot Finance.

**Q: Explain Priority Sector Lending and its importance.**
A: PSL mandates banks to direct a minimum 40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC) to specified sectors: Agriculture (18%), MSMEs, Education, Housing (affordable), Renewable Energy, Social Infrastructure, Export Credit, Weaker Sections (10%). Rationale: credit doesn't naturally flow to these sectors due to higher risk and lower returns. Banks that fail PSL targets must invest shortfall in RIDF (Rural Infrastructure Development Fund) at low interest rates. PSL drives financial inclusion and rural development.

**Q: What is Financial Inclusion and what initiatives has India taken?**
A: Financial inclusion = ensuring all individuals and businesses have access to affordable financial products. India's initiatives: PM Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY) — zero-balance accounts with RuPay cards; PM Mudra Yojana — collateral-free loans up to ₹10 lakh for MSMEs; Atal Pension Yojana; PMSBY, PMJJBY (insurance schemes); BC (Business Correspondent) model for banking the unbanked. Results: 500M+ Jan Dhan accounts opened; account ownership rose from 35% (2011) to 80%+ (2024).
**Q: What is the difference between SBI PO and IBPS PO exams?**
SBI PO is conducted by State Bank of India for its own Probationary Officer recruitment. IBPS PO is conducted by the Institute of Banking Personnel Selection for participating public sector banks (excluding SBI). Both have similar structure: Prelims (Reasoning, Quant, English) + Mains (Data Analysis, Reasoning/Computer, General/Economy/Banking Awareness, English) + Interview/GD. SBI PO is considered more prestigious and slightly harder. IBPS PO results in placement across multiple banks.

**Q: What topics are covered under Banking Awareness?**
RBI structure and monetary policy tools (repo rate, reverse repo, CRR, SLR, MSF), types of banks (commercial, cooperative, RRBs, foreign banks), government schemes (Jan Dhan, MUDRA, PM SVANidhi), financial inclusion, NBFC regulations, payment systems (NEFT, RTGS, IMPS, UPI differences), Basel norms, NPA classification, SARFAESI Act, and current affairs related to banking and economy.
