# First Aid Interview Q&A

**Q: When should you call 112 vs handle it yourself?**
Call 112 immediately for: unconsciousness, absent or abnormal breathing, severe uncontrolled bleeding, suspected heart attack or stroke, anaphylaxis (any sign), serious burns, suspected spinal injury, drowning, major trauma, overdose.
Handle yourself: minor cuts, small superficial burns (not on face/hands), mild sprains, brief fainting with quick recovery, minor nosebleeds.
When in doubt — always call 112. You cannot make the situation worse by calling.

**Q: How do you know if CPR is effective?**
In real-time, you cannot reliably verify effectiveness. Focus on correct technique: heel of hand on center of chest, arms straight, compress ≥5 cm (2 inches), rate 100-120/min, allow full chest recoil between compressions, minimize interruptions (<10 sec). Signs of return of circulation: spontaneous breathing, movement, improved skin colour. Attach AED immediately when available — it will assess cardiac rhythm objectively.

**Q: When is it dangerous to move someone with a possible spinal injury?**
Moving is dangerous and should be avoided unless there is immediate life threat (fire, flood, drowning) or CPR is impossible in current position. If must move: keep head-neck-spine perfectly aligned as one rigid unit — log roll technique with 3+ people. Always suspect spinal injury in: high-speed collisions, falls from height, diving accidents, significant head or neck trauma.

**Q: What is the recovery position and when is it used?**
Left lateral (three-quarter prone) position for unconscious patient who IS breathing normally. Prevents tongue blocking the airway and prevents aspiration of vomit.
Placement: roll to side, top knee bent forward for stability, top arm supports head, tilt head back slightly to keep airway open.
Do NOT use if suspected spinal injury. Monitor breathing continuously. Start CPR immediately if they stop breathing.

**Q: What does India's Good Samaritan law protect?**
The Supreme Court Guidelines (2015) and Motor Vehicles Act (2019): protects bystanders who provide emergency assistance to road accident victims in good faith. Protections: no compulsion to become a witness, immunity from civil and criminal liability, protection from police harassment. You can help without fear of legal consequences as long as you act in good faith.

**Q: Why is epinephrine given first in anaphylaxis, not antihistamines?**
Anaphylaxis causes life-threatening bronchospasm, cardiovascular collapse, and laryngeal oedema within minutes. Antihistamines take 30-60 minutes to act and only block histamine — they do NOT reverse bronchospasm or restore blood pressure.
Epinephrine acts within 1-2 minutes: reverses bronchospasm (beta-2 agonism), increases blood pressure (alpha-1 vasoconstriction), reduces laryngeal oedema (alpha-1). Delay in epinephrine = preventable death. Antihistamines are adjuncts only — NEVER the primary treatment for anaphylaxis.

**Q: How do you perform the Heimlich manoeuvre correctly on an adult?**
Stand behind the conscious choking person. Place one foot forward for stability. Make a fist with one hand and place it thumb-side against the abdomen, just above the navel and well below the breastbone. Grasp your fist with the other hand. Give firm upward-and-inward thrusts (J-motion). Continue until object is expelled or person becomes unconscious. If unconscious: lower to floor, call 112, begin CPR. Before each breath in CPR: look in mouth and remove visible object only — do not do blind finger sweeps.

**Q: How do you recognise and treat shock?**
Recognition: pale/cold/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, anxiety or confusion, dizziness or fainting, decreased urine output. Causes: severe blood loss, severe dehydration, serious infection (septic shock), heart failure (cardiogenic), severe allergic reaction (anaphylactic). First aid: call 112 immediately. Lay person down, raise legs 30 cm if no spinal injury or breathing difficulty (helps blood return to heart). Keep warm with blanket. Do not give anything to eat or drink. Monitor breathing continuously. Do not leave alone.

**Q: What is the difference between a fracture and a sprain?**
Fracture: break or crack in a bone. Signs: pain, swelling, deformity, inability to use, bruising, sometimes bone visible or audible snap. Treatment: immobilise with splint in current position (don't realign), ice, elevate if possible, seek immediate medical care. Do not apply ice directly to skin.
Sprain: stretching or tearing of a ligament at a joint. Signs: pain, swelling, bruising, limited movement, no deformity. Treatment: RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation). Seek medical assessment if severe — cannot clinically distinguish grade 3 sprain from fracture without X-ray.

**Q: What is the difference between heat exhaustion and heat stroke?**
Heat exhaustion: body overheating but temperature regulation still functioning. Signs: heavy sweating, cool/pale/clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, dizziness, headache, fainting, muscle cramps, core temperature <40°C. Treatment: move to cool place, lie down and elevate legs, remove excess clothing, cool with wet cloths, rehydrate with water or electrolyte drink. Usually resolves with treatment.
Heat stroke: MEDICAL EMERGENCY — body temperature regulation has failed. Core temperature >40°C. Signs: hot/red/dry or damp skin (not sweating), rapid strong pulse, confusion/altered consciousness, loss of consciousness. Treatment: call 112 IMMEDIATELY. Cool by any means possible: ice packs to neck/armpits/groin, cool water immersion if available, fan with misting. Do not give fluids if unconscious. Heat stroke can be fatal within minutes if untreated.

**Q: How do you treat a severe nosebleed?**
Sit upright and lean SLIGHTLY FORWARD (not back — avoids blood going into throat/stomach). Pinch soft part of nose (below bone) firmly. Breathe through mouth. Hold for 10-15 minutes without releasing. Apply cold compress to bridge of nose. DO NOT: tilt head back, stuff tissue far into nostril, check if bleeding stopped by releasing before 10 min. Seek medical care if: bleeding doesn't stop after 20 minutes, occurs after head injury, very heavy flow, taking blood thinners, or recurrent. If unconscious: lay in recovery position (blood drains from mouth).

**Q: What is the difference between a minor cut and a wound that needs medical attention?**
Minor cut (handle yourself): superficial, edges close together, bleeding stops with pressure within 10 minutes, no signs of infection, not on face or over a joint, not caused by a bite or rusty object.
Seek medical care for: bleeding not controlled after 10 minutes of direct pressure, wound edges gapping >1cm or won't stay closed, deep wounds (through all skin layers), wounds on face (for optimal cosmetic outcome), over joints (need to check for tendon involvement), caused by human or animal bite (infection risk), caused by dirty or rusty object (tetanus risk), any wound with signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaks, fever).
