# Personal Finance Interview Q&A

**Q: How much life insurance do I need?**
Rule of thumb: 10-15× annual income + all outstanding liabilities (home loan, car loan, business loan) + major future commitments (children's education, wedding).
Example: Annual income ₹15L × 15 = ₹2.25 crore + home loan ₹50L = ₹2.75 crore minimum.
Use pure term insurance — cheapest way to get large coverage. ₹1 crore cover for a healthy 30-year-old costs approximately ₹10,000-15,000/year. Avoid investment-cum-insurance products (ULIPs, endowment) — high charges and poor investment returns.

**Q: SIP vs lump sum — when is each better?**
SIP: fixed amount invested at regular intervals (monthly). Benefits: rupee cost averaging (buys more units when markets fall), builds discipline, no need to time the market, accessible for salaried individuals. Best for: most investors, long-term wealth building.
Lump sum: entire amount invested at once. Potentially better if markets are at historically attractive valuations. Requires market timing skill — difficult to execute consistently. Best for: when you receive a large windfall (bonus, inheritance) and valuations look reasonable.
For most people, SIP is lower-risk and equally effective long-term.

**Q: Old vs new income tax regime — how do I choose?**
Calculate your total deductions under old regime: 80C (₹1.5L) + HRA + home loan interest (₹2L) + 80D + 80CCD(1B) (₹50K) + standard deduction (₹50K) + others. If total deductions exceed approximately ₹3.5-4L, old regime typically wins. If fewer deductions (simpler life, no home loan, limited investments), new regime often saves tax with lower slab rates. You can switch every year as a salaried employee — calculate both scenarios each year.

**Q: What is the CIBIL score and how do I improve it?**
Credit score from CIBIL (or Experian, Equifax, CRIF) ranging 300-900. Above 750 = good; enables loan approvals at better interest rates. Built from your credit history reported by lenders.
To maintain/improve: pay ALL EMIs and credit card bills on time (most important — 35% of score), keep credit card utilisation below 30% of limit, don't apply for multiple loans simultaneously, don't close old credit cards (length of history matters), maintain a mix of credit types (cards + loan).

**Q: What is the 4% withdrawal rule?**
A retirement corpus withdrawal guideline: you can withdraw 4% of your total corpus annually and it should last 30 years (based on historical market data). Corpus needed = Annual expenses / 0.04 = Annual expenses × 25 = Monthly expenses × 300.
For India: some financial planners recommend 3-3.5% withdrawal rate due to higher inflation. Example: need ₹70,000/month → ₹70,000 × 300 = ₹2.1 crore corpus needed.

**Q: Index fund vs actively managed fund for long-term investors?**
Decades of global data show: 70-80% of actively managed equity funds underperform their benchmark index over 10+ years, after accounting for expense ratios (1-2.5% annually vs 0.1-0.2% for index funds). The 1-2% annual cost difference compounds massively over 20-30 years. Most evidence-based recommendation for long-term retail investors: Nifty 50 or Nifty 500 low-cost index fund as core equity holding. Active funds may add value in less efficient market segments (mid/small-cap) if the fund manager has demonstrated genuine consistent skill over a full market cycle.

**Q: What is a mutual fund expense ratio and why does it matter?**
Expense ratio = annual fund operating costs as a percentage of assets under management (AUM). Deducted daily from NAV — you never see it directly but it reduces your returns. Example: ₹1 lakh invested for 20 years at 12% gross return: at 0.1% expense ratio (index fund) → ₹9.3 lakh. At 2% expense ratio (active fund with same gross return) → ₹6.7 lakh. The 1.9% difference in expense ratio = ₹2.6 lakh less wealth over 20 years. This is why low-cost index funds mathematically outperform most actively managed funds over long periods even if the underlying portfolio is identical.

**Q: What is rebalancing and how often should you do it?**
Portfolio rebalancing: adjusting your investment allocation back to your target when it drifts due to different returns across asset classes. Example: Target 70% equity / 30% debt. After a bull market: portfolio is 85% equity / 15% debt — more risk than intended. Rebalance by selling some equity and buying debt. When to rebalance: time-based (annually — simple, effective) or threshold-based (when any asset class drifts >5-10% from target — more precise). Tax consideration: rebalancing by redirecting NEW investments to underweight assets avoids triggering capital gains tax.

**Q: What is financial independence and how do you calculate your FI number?**
Financial independence: having sufficient passive income or invested assets that you no longer need active employment income to cover your living expenses. FI Number = Annual living expenses × 25 (using 4% safe withdrawal rate). This is your target corpus. Example: annual expenses ₹7.2 lakh (₹60,000/month) × 25 = ₹1.8 crore FI number. To reach it faster: reduce expenses (lowers FI number AND frees money to invest), increase income and invest the difference, maximise return through low-cost equity index funds. The FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) movement applies this framework.

**Q: What is the difference between term insurance and whole life insurance?**
Term insurance: Pure protection — pays a death benefit only if you die during the policy term (e.g., 30 years). No maturity benefit if you survive. Very cheap: ₹1 crore cover for a 30-year-old ≈ ₹10,000-15,000/year. Best value for pure life cover. Recommended by most financial advisors.
Whole life/endowment insurance: Combines insurance with an investment component. More expensive: same ₹1 crore cover + investment component may cost ₹80,000-1,00,000/year. Investment returns are typically poor (4-6% IRR after charges) compared to separate investments. Surrender charges if stopped early. ULIP (Unit Linked Insurance Plan): investment linked to equity/debt funds — similarly high charges reduce effective returns. Recommendation: buy term + invest the premium difference in mutual funds ('buy term, invest the rest').

**Q: How should you approach buying your first home — rent vs buy?**
Factors favouring buying: stable long-term income and location, property prices are not overvalued vs rent (check price-to-rent ratio — ideally <20), can afford 20%+ down payment without depleting emergency fund, plan to stay 7+ years (time needed to break even vs renting after transaction costs). Factors favouring renting: mobile career, city is expensive relative to rents, better investment opportunities exist, you value flexibility, total cost of ownership exceeds rental equivalent. India note: unlike the West, homes in India often appreciate faster than inflation historically — but EMI vs rent comparison must include the opportunity cost of the down payment invested elsewhere. Neither is universally better — it depends on personal circumstances and local market conditions.

**Q: What is SIP top-up and why is it useful?**
SIP top-up (step-up SIP): automatically increasing your SIP amount at regular intervals (annually typically), usually by a fixed amount or percentage. Example: Start ₹5,000/month SIP, increase by ₹1,000 each year. Year 1: ₹5,000, Year 2: ₹6,000, Year 3: ₹7,000, etc. Why useful: income typically grows over time — increasing SIP in line with income growth accelerates wealth building dramatically. A ₹5,000 SIP for 20 years at 12% = ~₹49 lakh. The same SIP with 10% annual step-up = ~₹90 lakh. Most mutual fund platforms (Zerodha Coin, Kuvera, Groww, directly on AMC websites) offer step-up SIP functionality.
