### The Power of Compounding
Rule of 72: Years to double = 72 ÷ interest rate.
₹10,000/month SIP at 12% return:
- 20 years → approximately ₹1 crore
- 30 years → approximately ₹3.5 crore
- 35 years → approximately ₹6.5 crore
The extra 10-15 years of compounding is exponential — time is the only factor money cannot buy.

### Mutual Funds
Pooled investment vehicle; professional management; SEBI-regulated.
Categories:
- Equity funds: stocks; large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap, flexi-cap, sectoral, thematic
- Debt funds: bonds and money market instruments; lower risk; better than FD post-tax for 3+ year holding
- Hybrid/balanced funds: mix of equity and debt; automatic rebalancing
- Index funds: passively track an index (Nifty 50, Sensex); expense ratio 0.1-0.2%; most cost-efficient

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan): fixed monthly amount invested automatically. Rupee cost averaging: buys more units when prices fall, fewer when prices rise. Removes need to time the market. Most effective long-term equity wealth-building tool for salaried individuals.

## Tax Planning (India)

### Section 80C (Max ₹1.5 lakh deduction)
PPF (15-year lock-in; 7.1%; tax-free EEE status), ELSS (3-year lock-in; market-linked; LTCG taxed at 10% above ₹1L), NSC (5-year; 7.7%; taxable), 5-year tax-saving FD (~6.5%; taxable), life insurance premiums, NPS contributions.

### Additional Deductions
80D: Health insurance premium (₹25,000 self/family + ₹25,000 parents; ₹50,000 if parents are senior citizens).
80CCD(1B): NPS additional contribution ₹50,000 (OVER AND ABOVE 80C limit — net saving up to ₹15,600 extra at 30% tax bracket).
HRA: Exempt from tax if renting; calculate using HRA received, actual rent paid, and 10%/40%/50% of basic salary formula.
Standard deduction: ₹50,000 for all salaried employees (automatic; no documentation needed).

## Building Wealth — Practical Steps

### Priority Sequence
1. Emergency fund (3-6 months expenses) — in savings account + liquid fund
2. Adequate term life insurance — ₹1 crore minimum if dependents
3. Health insurance — ₹5-10 lakh family floater minimum
4. Pay off high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans) — guaranteed tax-free 24-48% return
5. Tax-efficient investments (80C — PPF or ELSS; 80D; NPS)
6. Long-term equity SIPs (Nifty 50 index fund as core holding)
7. Additional goals (child education, house down payment, retirement top-up)

### Emergency Fund Location
50% in savings account (instant T+0 access).
50% in liquid mutual fund (T+1 redemption; 7-7.5% return; better than savings account).
Do NOT invest emergency fund in equities — they can fall 30-50% exactly when you need the money most.

**Q: What is the difference between growth and dividend options in mutual funds?**
Growth option: profits and gains are reinvested within the fund, increasing the NAV. No cash flows to the investor. Better for long-term wealth compounding — reinvested returns generate more returns. Tax: pay capital gains tax only when you sell (LTCG 10% above ₹1L for equity held >1 year).
Dividend option (now called IDCW — Income Distribution cum Capital Withdrawal): fund periodically distributes money from NAV. The NAV falls by the distribution amount. Taxed as income in your hands at your slab rate (no longer a 'tax-free dividend'). For most long-term investors: growth option is better — no tax drag until you choose to sell.
