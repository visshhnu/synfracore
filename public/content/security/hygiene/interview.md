# Personal Hygiene Interview Q&A

**Q: How often should you shower?**
Once daily is appropriate for most people and climates. Always shower promptly after heavy exercise or sweating. Over-showering (multiple times daily) strips natural skin oils, disrupts skin microbiome, and worsens eczema and dry skin conditions. Under-showering allows bacterial overgrowth leading to body odour and fungal infections. Priority areas daily regardless of full shower: armpits, groin, and feet where bacteria accumulate fastest.

**Q: Is antibacterial soap better than regular soap?**
No. The FDA banned triclosan and other antibacterial ingredients from consumer soaps in 2016 — no evidence of greater effectiveness for everyday hygiene vs regular soap, plus concerns about contributing to antimicrobial resistance. Physical handwashing mechanism — friction + surfactant action + adequate water + ≥20 seconds — removes pathogens effectively. Technique and duration matter far more than whether soap is antibacterial.

**Q: How should you clean your ears?**
The ear canal is self-cleaning — earwax migrates outward naturally via jaw movements. Correct approach: clean only the outer ear (pinna) with a damp cloth during bathing. Never insert cotton swabs into the ear canal — they compact wax deeper, can damage the eardrum, and increase infection risk (otitis externa). For earwax buildup causing symptoms: OTC earwax softening drops (olive oil, carbamide peroxide), then gentle warm water rinse, or see a healthcare provider for microsuction.

**Q: What causes bad breath (halitosis) and how is it treated?**
80% originates from the back of the tongue where anaerobic bacteria produce volatile sulphur compounds (hydrogen sulphide, methyl mercaptan). Other causes: gum disease (periodontal pockets harbour bacteria), dry mouth (saliva suppresses bacteria), post-nasal drip, acid reflux, certain foods (garlic, onion), smoking, kidney/liver disease, or poorly controlled diabetes.
Treatment: tongue scraping (most effective single intervention), thorough flossing, staying well hydrated, treating gum disease, addressing dry mouth, managing systemic conditions.

**Q: Can sharing towels spread infections?**
Yes. Towels can harbour: skin bacteria (Staphylococcus aureus including MRSA), fungi causing athlete's foot (Tinea pedis) and ringworm (Tinea corporis), and viruses (molluscum contagiosum, conjunctivitis). Sharing directly transmits these. Rules: one towel per person, wash every 3-4 uses, hang spread out to dry fully between uses (bunched damp towels develop mould and bacteria within hours). Musty smell = contaminated — wash immediately at 60°C+.

**Q: What is the correct tooth brushing technique?**
Use a soft-bristled toothbrush at 45° to the gum line. Gentle small circular or back-and-forth strokes — no aggressive scrubbing (causes gum recession and enamel erosion). Cover all surfaces: outer, inner (use the brush tip vertically for front inner surfaces), and chewing surfaces. Duration: 2 full minutes (most people stop at 30-45 seconds). After brushing: spit the toothpaste but do NOT rinse with water — fluoride needs to remain on teeth to remineralise enamel. Replace brush every 3 months or immediately after illness.

**Q: What causes foot odour and how do you prevent it?**
Foot odour caused by bacteria (Brevibacterium, Staphylococcus) on skin breaking down sweat into isovaleric acid (cheesy smell). Feet have more sweat glands per cm² than anywhere else on the body. Prevention: wash feet daily with soap between toes, dry thoroughly (fungi thrive in moisture), wear moisture-wicking socks (cotton or wool), rotate shoes (allow 24 hrs to dry), use antifungal powder if prone to athlete's foot, wear open shoes when possible. Foot sprays with aluminium chloride reduce sweating.

**Q: What is the correct way to wash hands before eating?**
Wet hands with clean running water. Apply soap (any type — antibacterial adds no benefit for everyday use). Lather for minimum 20 seconds: cover all surfaces including backs of hands, between fingers, under nails (most missed area). Rinse thoroughly under running water. Dry with a clean towel or air dry. Use paper towel to turn off tap if in public facility. The 20-second rule is critical — most people stop after 6 seconds which is insufficient to remove transient pathogens.

**Q: What causes foot odour and how do you prevent it?**
Caused by bacteria breaking down sweat into isovaleric acid on the skin. Prevention: wash feet daily with soap between toes, dry thoroughly after washing (fungi thrive in moisture), wear moisture-wicking socks, rotate shoes allowing 24 hours to dry, use antifungal powder if prone to athlete's foot.

**Q: How should you wash clothes to prevent skin infections?**
Wash underwear and gym clothes at 60°C or higher to kill bacteria and fungi. Use fragrance-free detergent if you have sensitive or eczema-prone skin. Avoid fabric softener on sportswear — it reduces moisture-wicking. Tumble dry or hang in sunlight when possible (UV is antibacterial). Never re-wear sweaty workout clothes without washing first.

**Q: What is proper nail hygiene?**
Fingernails: trim weekly, cut straight then file; keep clean under nail with a nail brush daily; avoid biting or tearing. Toenails: cut straight across (curved cuts cause ingrown nails); trim every 1-2 weeks; dry thoroughly after bathing. Signs of fungal infection: thickened, discoloured, crumbling nails — requires antifungal treatment (topical or oral depending on severity).

**Q: What is the microbiome of the skin and why does it matter?**
The skin hosts a diverse community of microorganisms (skin microbiome) — primarily bacteria (Staphylococcus epidermidis, Cutibacterium acnes, Corynebacterium), fungi (Malassezia), and viruses. These commensals: protect against pathogens by competing for resources and producing antimicrobial peptides, modulate skin immune responses, maintain skin barrier function (producing ceramides and short-chain fatty acids). Disruptions to skin microbiome: associated with eczema/atopic dermatitis (Staphylococcus aureus overgrowth), acne (Cutibacterium acnes dysbiosis), rosacea, and psoriasis. Over-cleansing, harsh antibacterial soaps, and frequent antibiotic use can disrupt the skin microbiome. Gentle cleansing preserves beneficial microbial communities.

**Q: What is the science behind deodorant vs antiperspirant?**
Deodorant: masks or neutralises body odour. Contains fragrances and antimicrobial agents (triclosan, zinc salts) that reduce odour-causing bacteria. Does NOT reduce sweating. Better choice for: sensitive skin, those who prefer to sweat naturally.
Antiperspirant: physically blocks sweat ducts using aluminium compounds (aluminium chlorohydrate, aluminium zirconium). Reduces sweat production. Concern about aluminium: very low systemic absorption through intact skin; no convincing evidence of link to breast cancer or Alzheimer's — regulatory agencies (FDA, EU) consider them safe. Both are effective for odour control; antiperspirant additionally reduces sweat. Apply at night to dry skin for maximum effectiveness.

**Q: How should you care for skin in different seasons?**
Summer/hot climate: lighter moisturiser (gel or lotion rather than cream); increase SPF frequency (reapply every 2 hours in direct sun); cleanse more frequently if sweating heavily; anti-chafing products for skin folds; shower after outdoor activities; drink more water; use antiperspirant.
Winter/cold/dry climate: switch to richer moisturiser (cream or ointment, especially for hands and lips); apply moisturiser immediately after bathing while skin is still slightly damp; use a humidifier indoors; avoid very hot showers (strips natural oils); protect exposed skin from wind (physical barrier — scarf for face, gloves for hands); SPF still needed — UV reflects off snow and winter sun at low angles is still damaging.
