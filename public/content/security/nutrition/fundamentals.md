# Nutrition Fundamentals

## The Six Essential Nutrients
1. Carbohydrates — primary energy source (4 kcal/g)
2. Proteins — structural and functional molecules (4 kcal/g)
3. Fats — energy storage, hormones, fat-soluble vitamins (9 kcal/g)
4. Vitamins — organic micronutrients needed in small amounts
5. Minerals — inorganic micronutrients (calcium, iron, zinc, etc.)
6. Water — solvent, transport medium, temperature regulation

## Carbohydrates
Simple carbohydrates: monosaccharides (glucose, fructose) and disaccharides (sucrose, lactose) — quick energy, rapid blood sugar rise.
Complex carbohydrates: starches (slow energy, gradual blood sugar) and dietary fibre (not digested by humans; fermented by gut bacteria).
Soluble fibre: oats, legumes, apple — lowers cholesterol, slows glucose absorption.
Insoluble fibre: whole grains, wheat bran, vegetables — promotes bowel regularity.
Recommended: 45-65% of total calories; prioritise whole food complex sources over refined.

## Proteins
Built from 20 amino acids; 9 are essential (must come from diet): histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine.
Complete protein (all 9 essential AAs): animal products (meat, fish, eggs, dairy), soy, quinoa.
Incomplete protein: most plant proteins — combine complementary sources (rice + lentils = complete protein pair).
Key functions: structural (muscle, collagen, keratin), enzymatic (all enzymes are proteins), hormonal (insulin, growth hormone), immune (antibodies), transport (haemoglobin).
Recommended: 0.8 g/kg body weight for sedentary; up to 2.2 g/kg for muscle building.

## Fats
Saturated: solid at room temperature; animal products, coconut/palm oil; limit — raises LDL cholesterol.
Monounsaturated: olive oil, avocado, almonds, cashews — heart-healthy; raise HDL.
Polyunsaturated omega-3: salmon, sardines, mackerel, flaxseed, walnuts — anti-inflammatory, essential for brain.
Polyunsaturated omega-6: vegetable oils — generally fine when balanced with omega-3.
Trans fats: artificial hydrogenation — strongly pro-inflammatory and atherogenic; AVOID completely; banned in many countries.
Functions: fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), hormone production, cell membrane structure, satiety.
Recommended: 20-35% of total calories.

## Vitamins — Fat-Soluble vs Water-Soluble
Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K): stored in body fat; toxicity possible with excessive supplementation.
Water-soluble (B-group, C): minimal storage; excess excreted in urine; daily intake needed.
Vitamin D: synthesised from sunlight; deficiency very common (indoor lifestyle, high latitude, dark skin, sunscreen); essential for calcium absorption, immunity, mood.
Vitamin B12: found only in animal products; vegans/vegetarians MUST supplement.

## Water — The Overlooked Nutrient
60% of adult body weight is water. Functions: temperature regulation (sweating), joint lubrication (synovial fluid), nutrient and oxygen transport, waste elimination, biochemical reactions.
Daily requirement: approximately 2.0-2.5 litres from all sources (food + drink). Increases with: exercise, heat, fever, breastfeeding.
Hydration indicator: urine colour. Pale yellow = well hydrated. Dark yellow/amber = dehydrated. Clear = overhydrated (dilutes electrolytes).

## Caloric Balance
Weight maintenance: energy in = energy out.
Weight loss: consistent caloric deficit (500 kcal/day ≈ 0.5 kg/week).
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate): calories needed at rest. Multiply by activity factor (1.2-1.9) for total daily energy expenditure.

**Q: What is the difference between macronutrients and micronutrients?**
Macronutrients: nutrients needed in LARGE amounts; provide energy. Three types: carbohydrates (4 kcal/g), proteins (4 kcal/g), fats (9 kcal/g). Alcohol also provides energy (7 kcal/g) but is not a nutrient.
Micronutrients: nutrients needed in SMALL amounts (milligrams or micrograms); do NOT provide energy but are essential for biochemical reactions, enzyme function, immune function, and development. Two types: vitamins (organic — can be destroyed by cooking, oxygen, light) and minerals (inorganic — not destroyed by cooking; leach into cooking water). Deficiencies in micronutrients cause specific diseases even when caloric intake is adequate ('hidden hunger').
