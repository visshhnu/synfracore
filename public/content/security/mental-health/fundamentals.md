# Mental Health Fundamentals

## What is Mental Health?
Mental health encompasses emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It shapes how we think, feel, act, handle stress, relate to others, and make decisions. Mental health exists on a spectrum — everyone experiences challenges at times. Mental illness is not weakness; it is a health condition with biological, psychological, and social determinants that responds to treatment.

## Common Mental Health Conditions

### Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
Core features: persistent low mood AND/OR loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia) for ≥2 weeks.
Associated symptoms: fatigue, sleep changes (insomnia or hypersomnia), appetite/weight changes, concentration impairment, feelings of worthlessness or guilt, psychomotor changes, suicidal ideation.
Prevalence: affects approximately 280 million people globally; ~7-8% lifetime prevalence.
Treatment: CBT (psychotherapy), SSRIs/SNRIs (medication), exercise, or combination. Combination most effective for moderate-severe.

### Anxiety Disorders
GAD (Generalised Anxiety Disorder): excessive, difficult-to-control worry across multiple life domains for ≥6 months.
Panic disorder: recurrent unexpected panic attacks (surge of intense fear with physical symptoms: palpitations, chest tightness, dizziness, shortness of breath, paraesthesiae).
Social anxiety disorder: significant fear of social situations and negative evaluation; leads to avoidance.
Specific phobias: intense, disproportionate fear of specific objects or situations.
Treatment: CBT with exposure therapy; SSRIs/SNRIs for maintenance; short-term benzodiazepines for acute crises only.

### Stress and Burnout
Acute stress: time-limited response to identifiable stressor; physiologically adaptive short-term.
Chronic stress: prolonged, without adequate recovery; damages cardiovascular, immune, endocrine, and gut systems.
Burnout (WHO ICD-11): occupational phenomenon from chronic unmanaged workplace stress. Three dimensions: emotional exhaustion, depersonalisation/cynicism, reduced sense of efficacy.

## Neurobiology Basics

### Key Neurotransmitters
| Neurotransmitter | Primary Roles | Low-level Association |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Serotonin | Mood, sleep, appetite, impulse control | Depression, anxiety |
| Dopamine | Reward, motivation, movement | Depression, ADHD, addiction |
| GABA | Inhibitory; calming | Anxiety, seizures |
| Norepinephrine | Alertness, stress response | Depression, ADHD |
| Glutamate | Excitatory; learning, memory | Anxiety; target of ketamine |

### HPA Axis and Stress Response
Stressor → Hypothalamus releases CRH → Pituitary releases ACTH → Adrenal cortex releases Cortisol.
Acute cortisol: beneficial — enhances memory consolidation, immune function, energy mobilization.
Chronic cortisol: damages hippocampus, suppresses immunity, promotes inflammation, disrupts sleep, causes HPA dysregulation.

## Biopsychosocial Model
Mental illness arises from interaction of:
- Biological: genetics, neurotransmitters, brain structure, hormone levels
- Psychological: cognitive patterns, early experiences, coping styles, personality
- Social: relationships, trauma, socioeconomic status, cultural context
Effective treatment addresses all three dimensions.

**Q: What is the role of sleep in mental health?**
Bidirectional relationship: inadequate sleep worsens virtually all mental health conditions; mental health conditions worsen sleep. During sleep: the brain consolidates memories and learning, emotional memories are processed and regulated (particularly during REM sleep), the glymphatic system clears metabolic waste products (including amyloid-beta — relevant to Alzheimer's risk), stress hormones are regulated. Sleep deprivation effects: impaired emotional regulation (increased amygdala reactivity, reduced prefrontal control), increased anxiety and depression symptoms, impaired cognitive function, increased risk of psychosis with severe deprivation. Recommendation: 7-9 hours for adults; consistent timing is as important as duration.
