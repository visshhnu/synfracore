# General Science Interview Q&A

**Q: Why does ice float on water?**
Water has an anomalous property: it expands when it freezes. Liquid water molecules are densely and randomly packed. When water freezes at 0°C, hydrogen bonds force molecules into a rigid hexagonal lattice with more space between molecules than in liquid water. Ice density ≈ 0.917 g/cm³ vs liquid water ≈ 1.00 g/cm³. Since ice is less dense, it floats. Critical biological consequence: ice forms an insulating layer on the surface of lakes and ponds, allowing aquatic life to survive winter beneath the liquid water layer.

**Q: Explain the greenhouse effect and global warming.**
Natural greenhouse effect: Solar radiation (short-wave) passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth's surface. The surface re-emits heat as infrared radiation (long-wave). Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, H₂O vapour, CFCs) absorb this infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, warming the lower atmosphere. Without this natural GHE, Earth's average temperature would be approximately −18°C instead of +15°C — life as we know it would be impossible.
Enhanced GHE: Human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture, industry) have increased atmospheric CO₂ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm (2023). This traps more heat → global warming → climate change with cascading effects.

**Q: Explain DNA replication — why semi-conservative?**
Semi-conservative replication: each new DNA double helix contains one original template strand and one newly synthesised strand. Proven by Meselson-Stahl experiment (1958) using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N density labelling.
Key enzymes: Helicase (unwinds double helix), Primase (adds RNA primer), DNA Polymerase III (synthesises new strand 5'→3' using template), DNA Polymerase I (removes primers), DNA Ligase (joins Okazaki fragments on lagging strand), Proofreading activity reduces error rate to ~1 per 10⁹ bases.

**Q: What is osmosis and where does it occur biologically?**
Osmosis: net movement of water molecules through a selectively permeable membrane from a region of HIGH water potential (LOW solute concentration) to LOW water potential (HIGH solute concentration), until equilibrium is reached.
Biological examples: root hair cells absorbing water from soil; plant cell turgor pressure (prevents wilting); kidney nephron reabsorbing water in collecting duct (ADH-regulated); erythrocytes in different tonicity solutions (crenation in hypertonic, lysis in hypotonic); food preservation by salt/sugar (creates hypertonic environment killing microbes by plasmolysis).

**Q: Why do metals conduct electricity while non-metals don't?**
Metals: metallic bonding — valence electrons are delocalised and free to move throughout the crystal lattice as a "sea of electrons." When voltage is applied, these free electrons drift in one direction, constituting electric current. Conductivity decreases with temperature (increased ionic vibration scatters electrons).
Non-metals: electrons are localised in covalent bonds or ionic bonds — no mobile charge carriers. Insulators. Exceptions: graphite (one delocalised π electron per carbon per layer), silicon/germanium (semiconductors — some delocalised electrons at room temperature, more at higher temperature, opposite to metals).

**Q: What is pH and why is blood pH so tightly controlled?**
pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]. Scale 0-14: below 7 = acidic, 7 = neutral, above 7 = alkaline. Each unit represents a 10-fold change in [H⁺]. Human blood pH 7.35-7.45 (slightly alkaline). This tight regulation (±0.05 units) is critical because: enzyme active sites are sensitive to pH changes (enzymes denature or lose activity outside optimal pH), haemoglobin oxygen affinity changes with pH (Bohr effect — acidosis shifts dissociation curve right), ion channel function is pH-dependent. Three regulatory systems: bicarbonate buffer (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻), respiratory regulation (breathing rate alters CO₂ and therefore carbonic acid levels), and renal regulation (kidneys excrete or retain H⁺ and HCO₃⁻ over hours to days).

**Q: How does natural selection work?**
Darwin's mechanism: variation (individuals within a population differ genetically), heritability (traits are passed to offspring), differential survival and reproduction (individuals with traits better suited to their environment survive and reproduce more than others). Over many generations: favourable traits increase in frequency in the population; unfavourable traits decrease. Natural selection is not "goal-directed" — it simply reflects differential survival and reproduction given current environmental conditions. Key insight: evolution is the cumulative result of natural selection acting on heritable variation across many generations.

**Q: Why do we need vaccines?**
Vaccines prime the immune system to recognise and rapidly respond to specific pathogens without causing disease. They introduce: killed or weakened pathogens, pathogen fragments (proteins/polysaccharides), or mRNA encoding a pathogen protein (COVID-19 vaccines). The immune system mounts a response and creates memory B and T cells. If exposed to the actual pathogen later, the immune system responds rapidly before significant illness develops. Herd immunity: when sufficient proportion of a population is immune, pathogen cannot spread effectively and even unvaccinated individuals are protected indirectly.

**Q: What is the difference between a hypothesis, theory, and law in science?**
Hypothesis: a testable, falsifiable prediction or explanation for an observation. Not yet tested or with limited testing. Example: 'Increased CO2 causes global temperature rise' (before testing).
Scientific Theory: an explanation supported by extensive evidence from repeated testing; can make predictions; NOT just a guess. Example: Theory of Evolution, Germ Theory of Disease, Atomic Theory.
Scientific Law: a mathematical description of an observed phenomenon; describes WHAT happens without necessarily explaining WHY. Example: Newton's Law of Gravitation, Boyle's Law. A theory does not 'become' a law — they answer different questions.

**Q: Explain nuclear fission and fusion and why fusion is the ideal energy source.**
Fission: heavy nucleus (uranium-235, plutonium-239) absorbs a neutron → splits into two medium nuclei + 2-3 neutrons + ~200 MeV energy. Neutrons cause more fissions → chain reaction. Used in nuclear reactors (controlled chain reaction) and atomic bombs (uncontrolled). Fuel is finite; produces radioactive waste with long half-lives; meltdown risk.
Fusion: light nuclei (deuterium + tritium, both hydrogen isotopes) fuse → helium + neutron + ~17.6 MeV. Powers the sun. Ideal energy characteristics: fuel from seawater (deuterium), no long-lived radioactive waste, no risk of runaway reaction (plasma is inherently unstable — reaction stops if conditions not maintained), enormous energy density (1 kg fusion fuel ≈ 10 million kg coal). Challenge: requires temperatures of 100+ million °C and containment by magnetic fields (tokamak) or laser compression — not yet achieved at net energy gain commercially. ITER project aims for Q=10 (10× energy out vs in) by 2035.

**Q: What is CRISPR and how does gene editing work?**
CRISPR-Cas9: a gene editing tool adapted from bacterial immune system. Components: guide RNA (gRNA) — a short RNA sequence designed to match the target DNA sequence; Cas9 — a nuclease (molecular scissors) that cuts the DNA at the location specified by the gRNA. Process: gRNA binds its complementary sequence in the genome → Cas9 makes a precise double-strand cut → cell repairs the cut (either disrupting the gene or inserting new sequence if a template is provided). Applications: treating genetic diseases (sickle cell disease, haemophilia — first CRISPR therapy approved by FDA 2023), developing disease-resistant crops (CRISPR-edited crops approved in US), cancer immunotherapy (editing T-cells), basic research (understanding gene function). Concerns: off-target edits, germline editing ethics, equitable access.

**Q: What is the difference between a conductor, semiconductor, and insulator?**
Based on electrical conductivity and band theory:
Conductor: valence and conduction bands overlap (no band gap) — electrons move freely at room temperature. High conductivity. Examples: copper, silver, gold, aluminium.
Insulator: very large band gap (>5 eV) — electrons cannot cross to conduction band at room temperature. Negligible conductivity. Examples: glass, rubber, wood, diamond.
Semiconductor: small band gap (0.5-3 eV) — at room temperature, some electrons have enough thermal energy to cross to conduction band. Conductivity between conductor and insulator; increases with temperature (opposite to metals). Examples: silicon (1.1 eV), germanium (0.7 eV), gallium arsenide. Properties can be tuned by doping (adding impurities) — basis of all transistors and electronics.
