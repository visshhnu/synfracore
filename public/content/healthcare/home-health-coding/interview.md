# Home Health Coding Interview Questions

## Common Interview Q&A

**Q: What is PDGM and how does it affect home health reimbursement?**

A: PDGM (Patient-Driven Groupings Model) replaced the old therapy-based home health PPS in January 2020. It changed payment from 60-day episodes to 30-day payment periods and removed therapy visit counts as a payment driver. Instead, each 30-day period is classified by five factors: admission source (community vs institutional), timing (early vs late), clinical grouping (12 groups by primary diagnosis), functional impairment level (from OASIS data), and comorbidity adjustment (based on secondary diagnoses). Accurate ICD-10-CM coding is now more critical than ever because the primary diagnosis directly determines the clinical grouping and thus reimbursement.

---

**Q: What makes a patient homebound for Medicare home health?**

A: Homebound status means leaving home requires a considerable and taxing effort due to illness or injury. The patient may leave briefly for medical appointments, religious services, or adult day care without losing homebound status. Documentation must support homebound in every certification period. Common supporting conditions: post-surgical recovery, severe COPD requiring oxygen, recent stroke with mobility deficits, unstable cardiac conditions, severe arthritis limiting ambulation, dementia with safety concerns.

---

**Q: What is OASIS and who completes it?**

A: OASIS (Outcome and Assessment Information Set) is a comprehensive patient assessment tool required for all Medicare home health patients. It is completed by a registered nurse or licensed therapist (not home health aide) at specific timepoints: start of care, resumption of care after inpatient stay, follow-up at 60 days, transfer to inpatient facility, and discharge. OASIS data drives PDGM case-mix grouping, quality measure calculation for Home Health Compare star ratings, and is submitted electronically to CMS.

---

**Q: How do you code a diabetic foot ulcer in home health?**

A: For a Type 2 diabetic patient with a foot ulcer, the combination code E11.621 (Type 2 DM with foot ulcer) is used as primary. An additional code from L97 is required to identify the site and severity of the ulcer. If the patient is on insulin, add Z79.4. If on oral antidiabetics, add Z79.84. The diabetes code always sequences first because it is the underlying cause, and the combination code includes the foot ulcer as a complication. Never code the ulcer separately from the diabetes when a combination code exists.

---

**Q: What is a LUPA and how can it be avoided?**

A: A LUPA (Low Utilization Payment Adjustment) occurs when a patient receives fewer visits in a 30-day period than the threshold set for their PDGM case-mix group. Instead of the full PDGM payment, the agency receives only a per-visit rate, which is typically much lower. LUPAs can be avoided by: identifying the LUPA threshold for each patient's group upfront, ensuring clinically justified visits are provided and documented, not discharging a patient prematurely when clinical needs exist, and addressing admission timing to avoid early discharges.

**Q: What resources do you use to stay current with coding changes?**

A: ICD-10-CM updates effective October 1 each year; CPT updates effective January 1. I monitor CMS transmittals for Medicare policy changes, read AHA Coding Clinic for ICD-10-CM guidance, CPT Assistant for CPT interpretation, and follow AAPC/AHIMA newsletters. I also review the OIG Work Plan annually to understand audit focus areas and attend webinars for continuing education units required to maintain certification.

---

**Q: Describe a time you found a coding error. What did you do?**

A: In a strong answer, describe discovering an upcoded E&M, a bundled procedure billed separately, or a diagnosis not supported by documentation. Explain: you verified the issue against guidelines, documented your finding, reported through proper channels (supervisor, compliance department), and helped identify whether it was systemic or isolated. Demonstrate that you understand the difference between error correction and whistleblowing, and that you follow proper escalation procedures.
