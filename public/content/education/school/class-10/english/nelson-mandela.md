# Nelson Mandela: Long Walk to Freedom

## Why This Chapter Matters
This autobiography extract from Nelson Mandela's "Long Walk to Freedom" is tested every year in CBSE Class 10 boards. Value-based questions, character analysis, and understanding of freedom struggle themes are the focus — 5-8 marks.

## About the Author
Nelson Mandela (1918-2013) — first black President of South Africa (1994-1999). Led the movement against apartheid (racial segregation). Spent 27 years in prison. Nobel Peace Prize 1993.

## Summary

The extract describes Mandela's inauguration as President of South Africa on 10 May 1994. It is a moment of historic transformation — a country moving from apartheid to democracy.

Mandela describes the ceremony in Pretoria. Dignitaries from 140 nations attend. Two deputy presidents are also sworn in. The South African military generals (who once enforced apartheid) salute the new black President — a remarkable reversal.

Mandela reflects on the journey — how his country has the most progressive Constitution in the world, guaranteeing freedom of all citizens. He thinks about the meaning of freedom: initially he thought of it as personal (running and swimming freely as a boy), then it narrowed to his community (when he realised black people had no freedom), then expanded to his life's work — freedom for ALL South Africans.

He acknowledges the heroes of the struggle who sacrificed their lives. He believes that oppression creates both oppressor and victim, and that liberating the oppressed also liberates the oppressor.

## Key Themes

**1. Freedom and its meaning:** Mandela's understanding of freedom evolves from personal to collective. He does not want freedom for himself alone — it is meaningless unless everyone is free.

**2. Courage and sacrifice:** The heroes he mentions died without seeing freedom. Their sacrifices made this day possible.

**3. Transformation:** A country built on racial inequality transforms into a democracy. The military generals who enforced apartheid now salute the man they once oppressed.

**4. Twin obligations:** Every person has obligations to family AND to community/nation. These twin duties define a meaningful life.

**5. The nature of oppressor and oppressed:** Both are robbed of humanity by apartheid — the oppressor loses his sense of humanity by denying others their rights.

## Important Questions with Answers

**Q1: What did being free mean to Mandela as a boy, young man, and mature man?**
As a boy: Freedom meant personal freedom — running freely, swimming in streams, roasting mealies, riding bulls.
As a young man: He realised that his people (black South Africans) were NOT free. His "transitory freedoms" were mere illusions. Freedom came to mean the right to live with dignity.
As a mature man: Freedom became inseparable from the freedom of his people. Individual freedom was meaningless without freedom for all.

**Q2: What is the "twin obligations" that Mandela mentions?**
Every person has obligations to (1) their family, parents, wife, children — a personal duty, AND (2) their community, country, and people — a social duty. In South Africa under apartheid, these two obligations conflicted because fulfilling one's duty to people meant being torn from family (prison, exile). Mandela and countless others chose to fulfil both, at great personal cost.

**Q3: Why did Mandela consider the oath-taking ceremony historic?**
Because it represented the first time in the history of South Africa that a black man took the highest office. It was the end of apartheid and the beginning of democracy. Leaders from 140 nations attended. Most importantly, the same military officers who once enforced racist laws now saluted a black President — a profound transformation.

**Q4: How did Mandela's understanding of freedom change over the years?**
Initially, freedom was a personal concept — the physical freedom of a young boy. Then it became a collective need when he understood his people's oppression. Finally, freedom became his life's purpose — not just for black South Africans but for all people, black and white, because true freedom requires liberating everyone.

**Q5: "The oppressor must be liberated just as surely as the oppressed." Explain.**
Mandela believes that apartheid dehumanises both the oppressed (who suffer) and the oppressor (who loses their own humanity by denying it to others). A man who denies freedom to others is himself a prisoner of prejudice and hatred. True liberation means freeing both — creating a society where all people are fully human.

## PYQs

**2023:** What did Mandela learn about courage from the brave men who struggled for freedom?
Mandela says courage is not the absence of fear — it is the triumph over fear. The brave man is not one who does not feel afraid, but one who conquers that fear. The heroes of the struggle showed courage by continuing to fight despite knowing the consequences.

**2022:** Describe the changes that the inauguration signified for South Africa.
It signified the end of apartheid and birth of a multiracial democracy. A black man became President. The same military that once enforced racial laws now served the new government. South Africa adopted the most progressive Constitution in the world, guaranteeing freedom to all citizens regardless of race.

**2021:** What is the meaning of apartheid?
Apartheid was a policy of racial segregation and discrimination enforced by the South African government from 1948 to 1994. Black South Africans were denied basic rights — to vote, to own land, to move freely, to access equal education. The word means "separateness" in Afrikaans.

Q5 (Hard). Why does Mandela say the oppressor must also be liberated?
Because by denying humanity to others, the oppressor loses their own humanity and is imprisoned by hatred and prejudice. True freedom requires ALL people to be freed — both from external oppression and from the prison of prejudice.
