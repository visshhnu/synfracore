# Power Sharing

## Why This Chapter Matters
Power Sharing is Civics Chapter 1 in Class 10 — one of the most scoring chapters. The Belgium and Sri Lanka case studies, forms of power sharing, and the prudential vs moral reasons are always tested. 4-6 marks.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Belgium vs Sri Lanka — The Two Case Studies

**Belgium:**
- Small country in Europe, complex ethnic composition
- 59% Dutch-speaking (Flemish), 40% French-speaking (Walloons), 1% German-speaking
- The French-speaking minority were economically and politically more powerful
- French speakers resisted any equal treatment
- Belgium solved it with innovative power sharing:
  - Equal ministers from French and Dutch communities in central government (even if unequal population)
  - Community governments with power over language, culture, education
  - Brussels (capital) has equal French and Dutch in municipal government

Result: Belgium avoided conflict and became federal successfully.

**Sri Lanka:**
- Sinhala-speaking majority = 74% | Tamil speakers = 18% (Sri Lankan Tamils) + 7% (Indian origin)
- After independence (1948), Sinhala-majority government passed laws marginalising Tamils:
  - Sinhala as only official language (1956 Act)
  - Preference to Sinhalese in university admissions and government jobs
  - Buddhism promoted officially
- Tamils felt alienated → movements for autonomy → violent civil war
- LTTE (Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam) fought for separate Tamil Eelam state
- Civil war lasted 26 years, ended 2009 with military defeat of LTTE

Result: Majoritarianism led to conflict, not unity. Sri Lanka is still healing.

**Key lesson:** Belgium accommodated differences → peaceful. Sri Lanka imposed majority will → conflict.

### 2. Why Power Sharing is Desirable

**Prudential reason (practical/wise):** Power sharing reduces conflict. If one group feels excluded and oppressed, they will resist — possibly violently. Sharing power keeps different groups satisfied and reduces social tension. It is SMART policy.

**Moral reason (right in principle):** Power sharing is the very spirit of democracy. Democracy means rule by the people — all the people. Excluding some groups contradicts the fundamental idea. It is the RIGHT thing to do.

### 3. Forms of Power Sharing

**Horizontal distribution:**
Power shared among different organs of government at the same level — Legislature, Executive, Judiciary.
Each acts as a CHECK on the others → system of checks and balances.
This prevents any single organ from exercising unlimited power.

**Vertical distribution (Federalism):**
Power divided between central (national) and state governments at different levels.
In India: Union → State → Local bodies (Panchayat/Municipality).
Higher levels cannot simply override lower levels — both have protected powers.

**Among different social groups:**
Reserved constituencies for scheduled castes and tribes in legislatures.
Community governments (like in Belgium) for different ethnic/language groups.
Ensures minorities are represented.

**Among political parties, pressure groups, and movements:**
Competition between parties → voters get choice → government is accountable.
Rights of political opposition → can question government, form alternative government.
Movements and pressure groups influence policy → power not monopolised.

## PYQs

**2023:** Explain with examples why power sharing is desirable.
Power sharing is desirable both for prudential and moral reasons. Prudentially: it reduces social conflict — in Belgium, equal power sharing among ethnic groups prevented civil war. Morally: it upholds the spirit of democracy — all people should have a say in governance. Majoritarianism in Sri Lanka, which excluded Tamils, led to a 26-year civil war. So power sharing makes societies both more stable and more just.

**2022:** What is the difference between prudential and moral reasons for power sharing?
Prudential reason: power sharing is WISE because it prevents conflict and ensures stability (practical benefit). Moral reason: power sharing is RIGHT because it upholds democratic principles of equality and self-governance (matter of principle). Belgium's approach is justified by both — it is both wise and democratic.

**2021:** What are the various forms of power sharing? Give one example of each.
Horizontal: between legislature, executive, judiciary (e.g., Parliament makes laws but courts can strike down unconstitutional ones).
Vertical: between central and state governments (e.g., India's Union-State-Local division).
Among social groups: reserved seats for SC/ST in Indian Parliament.
Among parties/movements: multi-party democracy where opposition can criticise the ruling party.
