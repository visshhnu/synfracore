# The Rise of Nationalism in Europe

## Why This Chapter Matters
This is the first chapter of History in Class 10 — one of the most important for CBSE boards. The French Revolution, Romanticism, nationalism movements, and key figures are tested every year. 5-8 marks minimum.

## Core Concepts

### 1. What is a Nation-State?
Before the 19th century, most of Europe consisted of kingdoms and empires with diverse populations. The concept of a nation-state — where people sharing common language, culture, and history govern themselves — was revolutionary.

### 2. French Revolution (1789) — Seeds of Nationalism
The French Revolution introduced new ideas: Liberty, Equality, Fraternity.
Key changes: abolition of monarchy, new laws applying equally to all citizens, standardised language (French), metric system, new French army (citizens' army, not mercenary).

The French armies carried the idea of nationalism across Europe — people in other countries saw that ordinary citizens could overthrow kings and govern themselves.

### 3. Napoleonic Code (1804)
Napoleon spread revolutionary ideas across Europe through the Napoleonic Code:
- Equality before the law
- Right to property
- Abolition of feudal system
- Freedom of religion

However: Napoleon was also an autocrat who raised taxes, imposed censorship, and treated conquered regions as his own. Initially welcomed as liberator, later seen as conqueror.

### 4. Conservatism After Napoleon
After Napoleon's defeat, European rulers tried to restore the old order at the Congress of Vienna (1815). Conservatives believed in traditional institutions — monarchy, Church, social hierarchy.

But nationalism could not be suppressed. A series of revolutions followed.

### 5. Role of Culture — Romanticism
Romanticism was a cultural movement that sought to create a sense of national identity through:
- Folk songs, poetry, and dances (collected and popularised)
- Local languages instead of Latin
- Paintings of national landscapes
- Stories and legends — Brothers Grimm collected German folk tales

Key figure: Johann Gottfried Herder (German philosopher) — argued that true German culture lives among ordinary folk people (das Volk). Culture, language, and identity are inseparable.

### 6. Hunger, Hardship and Revolution (1830s-1848)
Economic hardships caused by bad harvests, rising bread prices, and unemployment created fertile ground for revolutions.

1848: Revolutions broke out across Europe. Middle classes (liberals) demanded constitutions and national states. BUT these revolutions mostly failed — rulers crushed them with military force.

### 7. Unification of Germany (Bismarck)
German states were many small kingdoms. Otto von Bismarck of Prussia unified Germany through "blood and iron" — three wars (against Denmark, Austria, France). In January 1871, the German Empire was proclaimed in the Hall of Mirrors, Versailles.

### 8. Unification of Italy (Mazzini, Cavour, Garibaldi)
Three main figures:
- **Mazzini:** Cultural nationalism, founded "Young Italy" — inspire through ideas
- **Cavour:** Political diplomat, PM of Sardinia — allied with France against Austria
- **Garibaldi:** Military leader — led the "Red Shirts" to capture southern Italy

Italy was unified in 1861.

### 9. Ethnic Groups and Nationalism's Dark Side
Nationalism also created problems — ethnic minorities within new nation-states were often oppressed. The Balkans (south-eastern Europe) became a powder keg because of competing nationalist claims — ultimately triggering World War I.

## Key Dates
1789: French Revolution
1804: Napoleonic Code
1815: Congress of Vienna
1831: Young Italy founded (Mazzini)
1848: Revolutions across Europe
1861: Italy unified
1871: Germany unified (German Empire proclaimed)

## PYQs

**2023:** Explain how Romanticism sought to create a sense of national identity.
Romanticism emphasized emotion, folk culture, and national heritage over reason and science. Romantic artists and writers collected folk songs, legends, and dances to create a shared national memory. They used vernacular languages instead of Latin, painted national landscapes, and glorified national heroes. This created emotional identification with the nation among ordinary people.

**2022:** What was the Napoleonic Code? How did it affect Europe?
The Napoleonic Code (1804) standardised French law — equality before law, right to property, freedom of religion, abolition of feudal privileges. When Napoleon conquered other European territories, he introduced this code, abolishing old feudal structures. This showed other people that change was possible and inspired nationalism.

**2021:** Why was the 1848 revolution called the revolution of the "liberals"?
The 1848 revolutions were led by the educated middle class who believed in liberalism — individual freedom, constitutional government, and representative bodies. They wanted constitutions, end to autocratic rule, and national self-determination. However, they failed because rulers used military force and the revolutions lacked mass support.

**2020:** Explain the role of women in nationalist struggles in Europe.
Women formed their own political associations, founded newspapers, and participated in revolutionary movements. However, they were denied suffrage and political rights in the new nation-states that emerged. They participated in building national culture — writing, painting, singing — while being excluded from formal political power.
