# Plant Kingdom

## Why This Chapter Matters
Plant Kingdom has 4-8 marks in NEET. Classification of plants (algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms), alternation of generations, and key characteristics of each group are tested.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Classification of Plant Kingdom

**Thallophyta (Algae):** No true stem, root, or leaves (thallus). No embryo.
Algae examples: Chlamydomonas (unicellular, green), Spirogyra (filamentous, green), Sargassum (brown, marine), Porphyra (red)

**Algae pigments:**
Green algae (Chlorophyceae): chlorophyll a, b | stored as starch
Brown algae (Phaeophyceae): chlorophyll a, c, fucoxanthin | stored as laminarin/mannitol
Red algae (Rhodophyceae): phycoerythrin, phycocyanin | stored as floridean starch

**Bryophyta (Mosses, Liverworts):**
Gametophyte dominant (haploid). Sporophyte dependent on gametophyte.
No true vascular tissue. No roots (rhizoids instead).
Examples: Marchantia (liverwort), Funaria (moss), Sphagnum (peat moss)
Archegonium (female) and Antheridium (male) sex organs.
Water required for fertilisation.

**Pteridophyta (Ferns):**
First true vascular plants (xylem + phloem). Sporophyte dominant (diploid).
No seeds. Reproduce by spores.
Examples: Selaginella, Salvinia, Pteris, Adiantum, Equisetum (horsetail)
Homosporous (one type of spore) — most ferns
Heterosporous (two types) — Selaginella, Salvinia

**Gymnosperms:**
Naked seeds (seeds not enclosed in fruit). No flowers.
Sporophyte dominant. Heterosporous (microsporangia + megasporangia).
Examples: Cycas, Pinus, Ginkgo, Welwitschia, Ephedra, Gnetum
Cycas: compound leaves; male and female cones on separate plants (dioecious)
Pinus: resin canals; male cone (microsporangiate strobili), female cone (megasporangiate)

**Angiosperms:**
Seeds enclosed in fruits. Flowers present. Most advanced.
Double fertilisation (unique to angiosperms).
Divided into Monocots and Dicots.

| Feature | Monocot | Dicot |
|---|---|---|
| Cotyledons | 1 | 2 |
| Venation | Parallel | Reticulate |
| Floral parts | 3 or multiples | 4 or 5 or multiples |
| Root system | Fibrous | Tap root |
| Examples | Grass, wheat, rice, maize, lily | Pea, bean, mango, sunflower |

### 2. Alternation of Generations

Gametophyte (n, haploid) → produces gametes → fertilisation → Zygote (2n)
Zygote → Sporophyte (2n, diploid) → meiosis → spores (n)
Spores → Gametophyte

**Dominant generation:**
Algae: Gametophyte dominant OR equal (depends on genus)
Bryophytes: Gametophyte dominant
Pteridophytes: Sporophyte dominant
Gymnosperms/Angiosperms: Sporophyte highly dominant (gametophyte is just a few cells)

### 3. Economic Importance

Algae:
- Agar-agar from Gelidium and Gracilaria (used in food and microbiology)
- Chlorella: single-cell protein
- Diatomite from diatoms (used in polishing, filtration)
- Sargassum: alginic acid production

Bryophytes:
- Sphagnum: fuel (peat), packing material, antiseptic

Pteridophytes:
- Azolla: bio-fertiliser (nitrogen fixation by symbiotic Anabaena)
- Salvinia: water pollution control

Gymnosperms:
- Pinus: turpentine, resin, timber
- Cycas: sago
- Ephedra: ephedrine (medicine)

## PYQs (NEET)

**NEET 2023:** Which group of plants shows heterospory?
Selaginella and Salvinia (pteridophytes). Also all gymnosperms and angiosperms are heterosporous.

**NEET 2022:** Alternation of generations where sporophyte is dependent on gametophyte:
Bryophytes — the sporophyte grows on the gametophyte and is dependent on it for nutrition.

**NEET 2021:** Agar-agar is obtained from:
Red algae — specifically Gelidium and Gracilaria.

**NEET 2020:** In gymnosperms, seeds are:
Naked (not enclosed in fruit/ovary wall) — that is why they are called gymnosperms ("gymnos" = naked).

Bryophyte: Gametophyte (dominant) → Sporophyte (dependent)
