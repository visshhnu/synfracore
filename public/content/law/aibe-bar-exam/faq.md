# AIBE FAQ

**Q: I cleared LLB last year. Am I eligible for AIBE?**
A: Yes, once you are enrolled with a State Bar Council, you can register for AIBE at any point. There is no time limit after enrollment.

**Q: Can I appear in courts before clearing AIBE?**
A: Technically no — a Certificate of Practice is required to practise. However, enrolled advocates can appear as juniors under a senior's supervision even before clearing AIBE in practice. BCI has clarified enrolled advocates can appear during pendency of AIBE attempt.

**Q: Is AIBE conducted online or offline?**
A: Offline — OMR-based MCQ paper at designated centres across India.

**Q: Where do I find AIBE previous year papers?**
A: BCI website (barcouncilofindia.org), AIBE official portal (bci-aibe.in), and various legal education platforms. BCI releases official answer keys after each exam.

**Q: What is the salary of a government advocate?**
A: Varies widely by state and level. Junior government pleaders: ₹30,000–₹60,000/month. Senior standing counsel at High Court level: ₹1–5 lakh per appearance. Additional Solicitor General (Central Government): ₹6.25 lakh per hearing (as per ASG pay scale). Government service lawyers (law officers in ministries): ₹7th Pay Commission pay scales (₹56,100–₹2,50,000 depending on post).

**Q: What is a Memorandum of Appearances vs Vakalatnama?**
A: A Vakalatnama is filed in most civil and criminal courts authorising an advocate to appear and act. In Supreme Court, a Memorandum of Appearance (MoA) is filed instead, which is simpler and does not require the client's signature — just the advocate's. Both serve the purpose of formally recording the advocate-client relationship before the court.

**Q: How much does a junior advocate typically earn in Year 1?**
A: ₹5,000–₹20,000 per month stipend from the senior, depending on city, law firm type, and senior's practice size. In Tier-1 cities (Mumbai, Delhi), law firm associates may start at ₹6–12 LPA through campus placement. Solo junior practice income is erratic in early years.

**Q: Can an advocate from one state practice in another state's courts?**
A: Yes — once enrolled with any State Bar Council, an advocate can practise in all courts and tribunals across India (Sec 30 Advocates Act), including High Courts and the Supreme Court. Some High Courts have their own enrolment requirements for regular appearances in that court.
