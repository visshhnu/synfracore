# Legal Fundamentals — Indian Legal System

## The Indian Legal System at a Glance

India follows a **common law system** inherited from British rule, blended with elements of civil law and personal laws. The Constitution of India (1950) is the supreme law — everything else flows from it.

---

## Hierarchy of Laws in India

Understanding the hierarchy is essential — a lower law cannot contradict a higher one.

```
CONSTITUTION OF INDIA  (Supreme — cannot be overridden)
        ↓
CENTRAL / PARLIAMENTARY LAWS  (Union List + Concurrent List)
        ↓
STATE LAWS  (State List + Concurrent List)
        ↓
DELEGATED / SUBORDINATE LEGISLATION  (Rules, Regulations, By-laws)
        ↓
EXECUTIVE ORDERS / NOTIFICATIONS
```

**Key principle:** If a State law conflicts with a Central law on a Concurrent List subject, the Central law prevails (Article 254).

## Court Hierarchy in India

```
SUPREME COURT OF INDIA  (New Delhi)
  — Final court of appeal
  — Guardian of Constitution
  — Original, appellate, advisory jurisdiction
        ↓
HIGH COURTS  (One per State / UT group)
  — Appellate court for district courts
  — Writ jurisdiction (Part III rights)
  — Supervisory jurisdiction over subordinate courts
        ↓
DISTRICT COURTS  (District level)
  — Civil: suits above ₹3 lakh (varies by state)
  — Criminal: Sessions Court for serious offences
        ↓
SUBORDINATE / LOWER COURTS
  — Civil Judge / Munsiff Courts (civil suits)
  — Magistrate Courts (criminal — Judicial + Executive)
  — Family Courts, Labour Courts, Consumer Forums
        ↓
TRIBUNALS & QUASI-JUDICIAL BODIES
  — NCLT, NGT, RERA, GST Tribunal, Armed Forces Tribunal
```

## Sources of Law in India

| Source | Examples |
|--------|---------|
| Constitutional Law | Articles 12–35 (Fundamental Rights), Part IV (DPSP) |
| Statutory Law | IPC 1860, CPC 1908, CrPC 1973, Contract Act 1872 |
| Personal Law | Hindu Marriage Act, Muslim Personal Law, Indian Succession Act |
| Customary Law | Local customs recognised by courts |
| Judicial Precedent | Supreme Court judgments bind all lower courts |
| Delegated Legislation | Rules, Regulations made under parent Acts |

## Key Branches of Law

### 1. Constitutional Law
Foundation of all Indian law. Covers Preamble, Fundamental Rights (Art 12–35), Directive Principles (Art 36–51), Fundamental Duties (Art 51A), and the structure of government.

### 2. Criminal Law
**IPC (Indian Penal Code) 1860** → now **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023**
- Defines offences and punishments
- Procedural law: **CrPC 1973** → now **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023**
- Evidence: **Indian Evidence Act 1872** → now **Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) 2023**

### 3. Civil Law
- **Contract Act 1872** — agreements, offer, acceptance, consideration, breach
- **Transfer of Property Act 1882** — movable/immovable property transactions
- **Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) 1908** — civil suit procedure

### 4. Family / Personal Law
- **Hindu Marriage Act 1955**, **Hindu Succession Act 1956**
- **Muslim Personal Law** (Shariat Application Act 1937)
- **Special Marriage Act 1954** (inter-faith/civil marriages)
- **Guardians and Wards Act 1890**

### 5. Property & Land Law
- **Registration Act 1908**, **Transfer of Property Act 1882**
- **Land Acquisition Act 2013** (Right to Fair Compensation)
- **RERA 2016** (Real Estate Regulation)

### 6. Labour & Employment Law
- **Industrial Disputes Act 1947**, **Factories Act 1948**
- **Labour Codes 2019–20** (consolidating 44 labour laws into 4 codes)
- **POSH Act 2013** (Sexual Harassment at Workplace)

### 7. Consumer Protection Law
- **Consumer Protection Act 2019** (replaced 1986 Act)
- Three-tier redressal: District → State → National Commission
- E-commerce sellers now covered

### 8. Intellectual Property Law
- **Patents Act 1970**, **Trade Marks Act 1999**, **Copyright Act 1957**
- **IT Act 2000** (cyber crimes, digital signatures)

## How to Identify Which Law Applies

**Step 1 — Nature of dispute:**
- Crime committed → Criminal law (BNS + BNSS)
- Contract broken → Contract Act / civil suit
- Property dispute → Transfer of Property Act / CPC
- Family matter → Personal law of the religion
- Consumer complaint → Consumer Protection Act 2019

**Step 2 — Which court has jurisdiction:**
- Crime: Magistrate → Sessions Court → High Court → SC
- Civil: Munsiff/Civil Judge → District → High Court → SC
- Consumer: District Commission → State Commission → NCDRC

**Step 3 — Remedies available:**
- Criminal: FIR → Charge sheet → Trial → Conviction/Acquittal
- Civil: Plaint → Summons → Evidence → Decree → Execution
- Consumer: Complaint → Mediation → Hearing → Order → Appeal

## FIR to Judgment: Criminal Complaint Flow

```
Incident occurs
    ↓
File FIR at Police Station (cognizable offence)
OR Complaint to Magistrate (non-cognizable offence)
    ↓
Police Investigation → Charge Sheet filed (within 60/90 days)
    ↓
Magistrate/Sessions Court takes cognizance
    ↓
Framing of Charges → Plea (Guilty/Not Guilty)
    ↓
Prosecution evidence → Defence evidence
    ↓
Arguments → Judgment
    ↓
Conviction → Sentence   OR   Acquittal
    ↓
Appeal: Sessions → High Court → Supreme Court
```

---

## Civil Suit Flow: Plaint to Decree

```
Wrong suffered (contract breach, property dispute, damages)
    ↓
Send Legal Notice (optional but recommended — 60 days for govt)
    ↓
File Plaint in appropriate Civil Court
    ↓
Summons issued to Defendant
    ↓
Written Statement by Defendant
    ↓
Issues framed by Court
    ↓
Evidence by both parties (documents + witnesses)
    ↓
Final Arguments
    ↓
Decree (in favour of Plaintiff or Defendant)
    ↓
Appeal: District → High Court → Supreme Court
```

## Frequently Confused Terms

- **Cognizable vs Non-Cognizable:** Police can arrest without warrant in cognizable offences (murder, robbery). Non-cognizable needs Magistrate's permission.
- **Bailable vs Non-Bailable:** Bail is a right in bailable offences. In non-bailable, court discretion applies.
- **Writ vs Appeal:** Writ is filed directly in High Court/Supreme Court for rights violation. Appeal challenges a lower court's order.
- **Civil vs Criminal Liability:** Same act can give rise to both (e.g., drunken driving causing death → criminal prosecution + civil suit for damages).
